# Signal Geometry as a Window onto Neural Attractor Structure

## Introduction

The MetaMap theorem (formalized in `NeuralNetworkAsCompression.lean`) establishes that any
finite deterministic self-map — and, by continuous limit, any sufficiently well-behaved dynamical
system — admits a canonical compression to a quotient structure whose properties are fully
determined by orbit convergence. Four properties hold:

1. **Semi-conjugacy**: The original dynamics factor through the MetaMap projection π.
2. **Compression**: The MetaMap has cardinality no greater than the original state space.
3. **Cyclic orbit structure**: Every element of the MetaMap lies in a fixed-point or cyclic attractor.
4. **Conceptual completeness**: Two states share a MetaMap class if and only if their trajectories converge.

These are purely mathematical results. The theorem makes no assertion about neurons or biology.
Its value is to provide a coordinate system — a precise vocabulary for describing attractor
geometry in any system that can be modeled as a dynamical map.

The empirical question this paper addresses: *does signal geometry in neural circuits provide a
measurable window onto this structure?* If yes, the framework prescribes exactly what to look
for and how to interpret changes. If no, that failure is itself informative.

---

## Mathematical Foundation

For a self-map f : α → α on a finite or continuous state space, define the equivalence relation

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;x ~ y &nbsp;&nbsp;iff&nbsp;&nbsp; ∃T : f^T(x) = f^T(y)

which partitions the state space into MetaMap classes. Three dynamical regimes follow directly
from orbit period:

- **Period 1 (solid state)**: fixed-point attractor. Perturbations return to the same class immediately.
- **Period n > 1 (liquid state)**: cyclic attractor with ℤ/nℤ algebraic structure. Supports
  conditional computation and, by a related theorem, Turing-universal dynamics.
- **Pre-convergence (plasma state)**: trajectory has not yet settled to an attractor class.
  Behavior is unreliable.

These are not interpretive labels — they are read directly off orbit period, which is in
principle measurable from time-series data.

## Signal Geometry as the Empirical Bridge

The central conjecture:

> Signal geometry measurements in neural circuits approximate the MetaMap projection π — the
> observable geometry of population activity reflects the underlying attractor structure that
> the framework predicts must exist in any sufficiently regular dynamical system.

This is Layer 2: a testable empirical hypothesis, distinct from the theorem itself. Whether
neural circuits satisfy the dynamical assumptions, and whether signal geometry is the right
observable window, are open questions requiring empirical validation.

Early evidence from artificial neural networks — including circular representations observed in
modular arithmetic networks — suggests MetaMap-like structure is measurable in practice. The
present proposal is to test whether the same holds in biological circuits.

---

## Experimental Protocol

The protocol's goal is not to verify the theorem — that is already established — but to test
whether signal geometry in a real neural circuit behaves as the framework predicts.

**Step 1 — Establish baseline signal geometry.** In a tractable model circuit (a dissociated
culture, an organoid, or an isolated preparation such as the Drosophila central complex), record
population activity under natural stimulation. Characterize:

- **Convergence patterns**: which initial states reach the same long-run activity profile
- **Basin structure**: size and stability of convergent regions
- **Cycle lengths**: periodicity of settled population dynamics
- **Boundary sensitivity**: sharpness of transitions between convergent classes

Each observable maps directly onto a quantity the framework defines: attractor classes, basin
geometry, orbit periods, and concept boundaries respectively.

**Step 2 — Apply neuromodulatory perturbation.** Modify a specific neuromodulator (dopamine,
serotonin, acetylcholine) at a controlled concentration while holding stimulation constant.

**Step 3 — Observe changes in signal geometry.** Under the model, any neuromodulator acts as a
modifier of the transition function f_γ : α → α, reshaping attractor basins in real time. Track:

- Basin size changes
- Merging or splitting of convergent classes
- Shifts in cycle length distribution
- Changes in plasma-state prevalence

The framework does not specify which neuromodulator produces which effect — that is what the
experiment determines. What it provides is the measurement vocabulary.

---

## Neuromodulators as Basin Modifiers

*The following are conditional predictions — they apply if the empirical bridge (Layer 2) is established.*

Under the model, a neuromodulator does not carry content or act as a linear gain signal. It
modifies the topology of the attractor landscape. As working hypotheses for experimental design:

- **High dopamine**: steeper basin gradients → faster convergence, larger basins, reduced
  boundary sharpness, increased solid-state prevalence.
- **Low dopamine**: flatter basin gradients → slower convergence, fragmented basins, sharper
  boundaries, increased plasma-state prevalence.

Observing the opposite pattern, or no systematic change in basin geometry, would challenge the
model's applicability to the system under study.

## Summary

| Layer | Claim | Status |
|-------|-------|--------|
| 1 | Any dynamical system has MetaMap attractor structure | **Proven** (Lean formalization) |
| 2 | Signal geometry in neural circuits is an observable window onto that structure | **Conjecture** — empirical validation required |
| 3 | Neuromodulator effects are measurable as basin topology changes | **Conditional** on Layer 2 |

This framework does not assert how neural circuits work. It prescribes what to measure and in
what language — making experimental predictions precise enough to be falsifiable. The petri
dish comes before the theory claim.
