Introduction

Neural networks, whether biological or artificial, can be formally understood as finite deterministic dynamical systems. Each forward pass in a network defines a self-map on a high-dimensional state space α:

f:α→α

Because α is effectively finite in any real system (discrete firing states, floating-point approximations), every trajectory under repeated application of f eventually settles into a cyclic orbit. By identifying states whose orbits converge, we construct a MetaMap quotient Q=α/∼, which captures the essential attractor structure of the network.

Mathematically, this yields four immediate consequences:

Semi-conjugacy: The original network dynamics factor through the MetaMap projection π:α→Q.
Compression: The MetaMap is strictly smaller than the full state space.
Cyclic orbit structure: Every element of Q lies in a fixed-point or cyclic attractor.
Conceptual completeness: Two states share a MetaMap class if and only if their trajectories converge.

From this perspective, what experimental neuroscientists observe as signal geometry—smooth trajectories, attractor basins, and sensitive boundaries—is not fundamental, but a continuous “shadow” of the discrete MetaMap structure. This formalism provides a directly testable hypothesis: by tracking convergence patterns, cyclicity, and basin stability in neural data, one can empirically reconstruct the underlying attractor geometry of the network.

In other words, the mathematics prescribes what to measure and where to look, rather than merely describing phenomena after the fact.

Neurotransmitters as Topographic Modulators

This framework immediately suggests a reinterpretation of neuromodulators:

For example, consider dopamine:

High dopamine: Steepens basin gradients, enlarging dominant attractor regions. Trajectories converge more quickly and may collapse previously distinct attractor classes, potentially leading to overgeneralization.
Low dopamine: Flattens basin gradients, making convergence slower and trajectories more sensitive to perturbations, consistent with distractibility or unstable representations.

This interpretation is experimentally actionable. By controlling neuromodulator levels in simplified systems—cell cultures, organoids, or isolated circuits—researchers can measure:

Trajectory convergence: How quickly different initial states reach the same MetaMap class.
Attractor cycle lengths: The periodicity of settled dynamics.
Basin stability: How sensitive attractor classes are to perturbations.

These observables are exactly those predicted by the finite dynamical system and MetaMap compression, allowing a direct mapping from neurotransmitter effects to underlying attractor geometry.

Predictive Principle

Rather than offering post hoc explanations, this model dictates the experimental protocol:

Identify convergent classes (π(x)=π(y)) through repeated sampling.
Measure periods and cycle structures in the MetaMap projection.
Apply neuromodulatory perturbations and track shifts in attractor topology.
Quantify basin stability and sensitivity to infer the effect of specific neurotransmitters.

This approach transforms the study of neuromodulation from descriptive neuroscience into quantitative, topologically guided measurement, grounded in rigorous mathematics. Signal geometry is not just a metaphor; it is the observable shadow of an underlying, testable attractor structure.
