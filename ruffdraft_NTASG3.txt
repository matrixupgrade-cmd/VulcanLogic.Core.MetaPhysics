Signal Geometry as a Window onto Neural Attractor Structure

Introduction

The MetaMap theorem (formalized in NeuralNetworkAsCompression.lean) establishes that any
finite deterministic self-map — and by continuous limit, any sufficiently well-behaved dynamical
system — admits a canonical compression to a quotient structure whose properties are fully
determined by orbit convergence. Specifically, four properties hold:


Semi-conjugacy: The original dynamics factor through the MetaMap projection π.
Compression: The MetaMap has cardinality no greater than the original state space.
Cyclic orbit structure: Every element of the MetaMap lies in a fixed-point or cyclic attractor.
Conceptual completeness: Two states share a MetaMap class if and only if their trajectories converge.


These are mathematical results, not biological claims. The theorem makes no assertion about neurons.
What it provides is a coordinate system: a precise vocabulary for describing attractor geometry in
any system that can be modeled as a dynamical map.

The question this paper addresses is empirical: does signal geometry in neural circuits serve as
a measurable window onto this structure? If it does, the framework prescribes exactly what to
look for and what changes mean. If it does not, that failure is itself informative.


The Mathematical Foundation

For any self-map f : α → α on a finite or continuous state space, the attractor equivalence
relation x ~ y (defined by eventual orbit convergence: ∃T such that f^T(x) = f^T(y)) partitions
the space into MetaMap classes. Three observable signatures follow directly from the orbit
period of each class:


Period 1 (solid state): fixed-point attractor. The system has an unconditional invariant
here. Perturbations return to the same class immediately.
Period n > 1 (liquid state): genuinely cyclic attractor with ZMod-n algebraic structure.
The system supports conditional computation and, by a related theorem, Turing-universal dynamics.
Pre-convergence (plasma state): the trajectory has not yet settled to an attractor class.
Behavior here is unreliable; the MetaMap class has not yet crystallized.


These three regimes are not interpretive labels. They are read directly off orbit period, which
is in principle measurable from time-series data.


Signal Geometry as the Empirical Bridge

The conjecture this paper proposes for empirical investigation is the following:


Signal geometry measurements in neural circuits are empirical approximations of the MetaMap
projection π — that is, the observable geometry of population activity reflects the underlying
attractor structure that the MetaMap framework predicts must exist in any sufficiently
regular dynamical system.



This is Layer 2 of the argument and it is a conjecture, not a consequence of the theorem. The
theorem guarantees that attractor structure exists in any dynamical system meeting the framework's
conditions. Whether neural circuits meet those conditions, and whether signal geometry is the
right observable window onto that structure, are empirical questions.

Existing work on signal geometry in artificial neural networks — including the observation of
circular representations in modular arithmetic networks — provides early and suggestive
evidence that MetaMap-like structure is measurable in practice. The present proposal is to
test whether the same holds in biological circuits.


Experimental Protocol

The proposed experimental approach follows directly from the framework's structure. The goal is
not to verify the theorem — that is already established — but to test whether signal geometry
in a real neural circuit behaves as the framework predicts it should.

Step 1 — Establish baseline signal geometry. In a tractable model circuit (a dissociated
culture, an organoid, or an isolated preparation such as the Drosophila central complex), record
population activity under natural stimulation. From this data, characterize:


Convergence patterns: which initial states reach the same long-run activity profile?
Basin structure: how large and how stable are the convergent regions?
Cycle lengths: is there periodic structure in settled population dynamics?
Boundary sensitivity: where are transitions between convergent classes sharpest?


These observables correspond directly to the MetaMap's attractor classes, basin geometry, orbit
periods, and concept boundaries respectively. The framework predicts all four should be
measurable; the experiment confirms or refutes that prediction.

Step 2 — Apply neuromodulatory perturbation. Modify a specific neuromodulator — dopamine,
serotonin, acetylcholine — at a controlled concentration while holding stimulation constant.

Step 3 — Observe changes in signal geometry. The framework predicts that any neuromodulator
acts as a modifier of the transition function f_γ : α → α, reshaping attractor basins in real
time. The specific direction of that modification is itself a testable hypothesis:


Do basins enlarge or contract?
Do previously distinct convergent classes merge?
Do cycle lengths increase or decrease?
Does pre-convergence (plasma-state) behavior become more or less prevalent?


The framework does not dictate which neuromodulator produces which effect. That is what the
experiment determines. What the framework provides is the measurement vocabulary: basin size,
cycle period, boundary sharpness, and convergence rate are the quantities to track, because
those are exactly what a change in f_γ would alter.


Neuromodulators as Basin Modifiers: Conditional Predictions

The following predictions are conditional on the empirical bridge (Layer 2) being established.
They describe what the framework would predict if signal geometry is confirmed as a reliable
observable of underlying attractor structure.

Under the MetaMap model, a neuromodulator does not carry content or act as a linear gain signal.
It modifies the topology of the attractor landscape — the shape and size of basins, the depth
of fixed-point attractors, and the period of cyclic orbits. As a working hypothesis for
experimental design:

High dopamine is predicted to steepen basin gradients, causing trajectories to converge more
rapidly and potentially collapsing previously distinct attractor classes. In signal geometry
terms: larger basins, shorter convergence times, reduced boundary sharpness between adjacent
classes, increased solid-state (period-1) prevalence.

Low dopamine is predicted to flatten basin gradients, slowing convergence and increasing
sensitivity to perturbations. In signal geometry terms: smaller or fragmented basins, longer
convergence times, sharper boundaries, increased plasma-state prevalence.

These are predictions in the framework's language, not assertions about known neuroscience.
They are stated here to make the framework falsifiable: if dopamine manipulation produces the
opposite pattern, or no systematic change in basin geometry, that is evidence against the
model's applicability to the system under study.


Summary

The argument has three layers with distinct epistemic status:

LayerClaimStatus1Any dynamical system has MetaMap attractor structureProven (Lean formalization)2Signal geometry in neural circuits is an observable window onto that structureConjecture — requires empirical validation3Neuromodulator effects are measurable as basin topology changesConditional on Layer 2

The framework's contribution is not to assert that neural circuits work a particular way. It is
to prescribe what to measure, given the conjecture that signal geometry is the right observable,
and to make the predictions specific enough to be falsifiable. The petri dish comes before
the theory claim.
