# Reading Neural Networks Through Signal Geometry
### A Practical Approach Grounded in Finite Iterative Systems

---

## The Core Reframe

Don't look at the weights. Look at what signals *do*.

A weight matrix tells you the recipe. Signal geometry tells you what the recipe cooks.
You can read every weight in a neural network and still have no idea how information
flows through it for a given input. The weights are anatomy. Signal geometry is physiology.

The starting intuition: imagine a Minecraft pressure plate connected to a network of
coupled rail carts. A mob steps on the plate — a binary signal — and that signal
propagates through the coupled carts, each affecting its neighbors. What emerges in
the carts is not binary. It's a probability field. A geometry. That geometry encodes
the mob, not because anyone programmed it to, but because the coupling produced it.

Neural networks work the same way. The geometry self-organizes from the couplings.
You need probes to read it.

**The MetaMap exists.** Any finite deterministic system eventually stabilizes into a

## The Three Behavioral States

Before probing, classify what you expect to find. The network's behavior at any
region of input space falls into one of three algebraic states:

**Solid** — Group-isomorphic rules. Always true regardless of input configuration.
These are the network's invariants: "this input type always produces this response
structure." Fully interpretable, fully stable. These are the fixed attractors.
Signal behavior: small perturbation in, small perturbation out. Consistent across
nearby inputs. The signal knows where it's going.

**Liquid** — Co-product of cyclic groups. True conditional rules that vary with
configuration, like a Rubik's cube — all states are valid, but which state you're
in depends on context. This is where the network's active computation lives.
Turing universal. Signal behavior: structured sensitivity, predictable variation
patterns, periodic or quasi-periodic response to systematic input sweeps.

**Plasma** — Semigroup non-convergence. The iterative rule never lands on a stable
pattern. High tension, no attractor for the current input. Signal behavior: chaotic
response to small perturbations, no stable signature across nearby inputs, high
sensitivity everywhere. This region requires external intervention — the network
is outside its trained basin.

## Concepts as Signal Geometry

A concept is a covering of input space — a grouping of points that gives them
shared meaning. The space of all possible concepts is 2^(N!) which is not
enumerable. You cannot find concepts by searching concept space.

You don't need to.

The signal geometry finds them. A concept, operationally, is a smooth region
of the signal flow field — a connected component where:

- Small perturbation in → small perturbation out
- Nearby inputs produce nearby activations at intermediate layers
- The Jacobian (sensitivity) is low and stable

Concept boundaries are where the geometry turns rough — high gradient, signals
diverge, the Jacobian spikes. Superposition (where the network encodes multiple
concepts in overlapping directions) lives at these boundaries — high gradient,
multiple competing smooth regions meeting.

The network did not define these concepts explicitly. They emerged as the stable
coverings of the signal flow. You read them by mapping the geometry, not by
searching the weight matrix.

## The Practical Probe Strategy

**Step 1 — Systematic input perturbation**

Take a base input. Apply small, systematic variations. Measure how the output
changes. Map ∂output/∂input across your input distribution.

Low sensitivity regions: you are in the interior of a concept. The network has
a stable, confident answer. The signal flows cleanly.

High sensitivity regions: you are near a concept boundary, or in a superposition
zone, or entering a plasma region.

**Step 2 — Layer-by-layer geometry mapping**

At each layer, measure:
- Do nearby inputs cluster together or diverge?
- Do the activation patterns stabilize across layers or keep changing?
- At what layer depth does the representation stop changing significantly?

The `semi-conjugacy` property tells you this must stabilize at some layer depth.
When it does, you have found the layer where the MetaMap has converged. That layer
is your most readable layer — the network has "made up its mind" by then.

**Step 3 — Look for cyclic structure**

Sweep inputs along systematic dimensions (brightness, rotation, frequency, etc.).
Watch the activation patterns at intermediate layers. Do they cycle? Do they
tile into repeating patterns?

If the network learned the algebraic structure of the problem, you should find
ZMod-n structure — cyclic repetition in activation space corresponding to
the cyclic structure of the domain. A network trained on modular arithmetic
should show circular structure. A network trained on rotation-invariant tasks
should show rotational periodicity in its intermediate representations.

This is empirically testable and is the bridge between the algebraic theory
and measurable network behavior.

**Step 4 — Blocking structure identification**

Some regions of input space will show trajectories (across layers) that never
escape a constrained subspace. These are blocking structures — the network
cannot represent concepts outside its trained distribution from within these
inputs.

Markers: activation patterns at later layers collapse to a small subspace
regardless of variation in the input. The network is "stuck" — not confused,
but structurally confined to a single causal branch.

These regions identify the boundary of the network's competence. Not failure
of intelligence — structural impossibility given the training distribution.

**Step 5 — Plasma region detection**

High sensitivity everywhere, no stable signature, chaotic response to systematic
sweeps, no convergence across layers. You are in a plasma region.

This is the network's honest confession: it has no reliable MetaMap for this
input. The appropriate response from the system is not to try harder. It is
to hand off — flag for human review, trigger a safe fallback, log for future
training.

The plasma detector is the most safety-relevant output of a signal geometry
analysis. It tells you where the network's behavior is unreliable before
something goes wrong, not after.

## What to Measure

| Measurement | What it reveals |
|---|---|
| Jacobian (∂output/∂input) | Smooth vs. rough geometry; concept interiors vs. boundaries |
| Layer-wise activation clustering | At what depth representation stabilizes (semi-conjugacy point) |
| Cyclic sweep response | Whether ZMod-n / cyclic group structure is present |
| Signature stability across nearby inputs | Solid vs. liquid vs. plasma classification |
| Activation subspace confinement | Blocking structures; distribution boundary |
| Cross-layer trajectory divergence | Constructive vs. destructive interference between input types |

## The Concept Composition Problem

When two concepts interact in the network, they are either:

**Constructively interfering** — their signal geometries are compatible, they
stay within a shared entropy budget, a single probe configuration handles both.
The network computes their interaction cleanly.

**Destructively interfering** — their trajectories push into new signature-space
regions neither handles alone. Complexity explodes. You need separate probe
configurations for each.

The constructive/destructive classification tells you which parts of the network
can be analyzed jointly and which must be analyzed separately. Trying to analyze
destructively interfering regions with a single probe framework is why global
interpretability is hard — you are forcing compatibility where there is none.

## The Validation Strategy

Start with networks trained on problems with known algebraic structure:

**Modular arithmetic** — the ground truth solution uses ZMod-n structure.
If the signal geometry shows circular/cyclic patterns in activation space,
the framework is confirmed. (Note: this has already been partially observed
empirically by other researchers, who found discrete Fourier structure in
transformers trained on modular addition — without the algebraic framework
to explain why.)

**Permutation problems** — ground truth is symmetric group structure.
Look for permutation-equivariant geometry in intermediate activations.

**Hierarchical classification** — ground truth is a tree/lattice structure.
Look for nested covering structure in the signal geometry.

In each case: if the network's signal geometry reflects the problem's algebraic
structure, the chain from neural network to finite iterative semigroup to
algebraic structure is empirically grounded.

## The Conservation Signature

In regions where the network has a stable, reliable circuit (solid state,
fully converged), there is a conservation property: the total "friction"
(accumulated signal change) over a complete cycle in the activation trajectory
is the identity. The signal returns to where it started.

This is not assumed — it is an algebraic consequence of being in a stable basin
with commutative group structure. You can test for it: probe the stable circuit
with inputs that form a natural cycle (rotate an image 360 degrees, traverse
a modular arithmetic cycle, etc.) and measure whether the activations return
to their starting configuration.

If they do: you have confirmed a solid-state, fully conserved circuit.
If they don't: you are in liquid or plasma territory.

## Summary: The Reading Protocol

1. **Sweep inputs systematically** — map the Jacobian across your input distribution.

2. **Classify regions** — solid (low, stable sensitivity), liquid (structured
   sensitivity), plasma (chaotic, unstable).

3. **Find the semi-conjugacy layer** — where layer-wise representation stops changing.
   This is your most readable layer.

4. **Map smooth regions as concepts** — connected components of low-sensitivity
   flow are the network's learned concepts. You did not enumerate them; the geometry
   found them.

5. **Look for cyclic structure** — systematic sweeps revealing ZMod-n patterns
   confirm the algebraic structure of what the network learned.

6. **Identify blocking structures** — regions where the signal never escapes a
   confined subspace. These are the distribution boundaries.

7. **Flag plasma regions** — these are where the network is unreliable. These are
   your safety-critical outputs.

8. **Test conservation** — cycle inputs through known cycles and verify activation
   return for solid circuits.

The network is not a black box. It is a finite iterative system with a provably
existing MetaMap. The signal geometry is that MetaMap made visible. You are not
reverse-engineering something arbitrary — you are reading a structured object
that algebraic theory says must have this structure. The geometry is real.
The probes just let you see it.
